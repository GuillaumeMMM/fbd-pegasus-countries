Israel

In 2016, the New York Times publishes a piece detailing how much Israeli company NSO Group would charge for its spyware Pegasus: a $500,000 installation fee, and $650,000 to spy on 10 iPhones or 10 Androids.

In 2018, a Qatari citizen and Mexican journalists and activists targeted using Pegasus sue NSO Group in Cyprus and in Israel, accusing the company of actively participating in illegal spying. According to the Associated Press, their lawyers are targeted by Black Cube (NSO Group co-founder Shalev Hulio later acknowledged to The New Yorker that “there was one involvement of Black Cube” for the lawsuit in Cyprus but denied NSO Group had hired Black Cube for other lawsuits).

In the aftermath of the publication of the Pegasus Project in July 2021, and according to unnamed sources cited by several Israeli media outlets, the Israeli government sets up a task force to manage the fallout of the revelations. NSO Group denies all allegations from the project and says Pegasus is being used legally. On July 22, the Israeli parliament announces it will set up an inquiry commission to investigate the allegations of misuse of Pegasus. On July 25, French president Emmanuel Macron, who is listed as a potential target by clients of NSO Group, asks Israel to investigate the Pegasus revelations. On July 28, Israeli defense officials visit NSO's offices as part of a government investigation into the company's actions. On July 29, according to an employee’s testimony to NPR, NSO Group temporarily blocks several government clients as the company investigates possible misuse.

In November 2021, the U.S. administration blacklists NSO Group. The same month, human rights organization Front Line Defenders reports that the phones of 6 Palestinian human rights defenders were hacked with Pegasus, the new CEO of NSO Group resigns just 12 days after his nomination was announced and Moody downgrades NSO Group’s credit rating to “poor,” deeming that the company is at a very high risk of breaching its debt agreement. Israel’s defense ministry also releases a new, shortened list of countries to which Israeli firms are allowed to export cybersecurity tools, reducing it from 102 countries to 37.

In early December, NSO Group announces it has opened an investigation after The New York Times reported Pegasus was used to spy on US diplomats in Uganda.

In January 2022, Israeli newspaper Calcalist reports that the Israeli police allegedly used Pegasus without judicial supervision or legal permission to spy on mayors, former government employees and leaders of protests against former Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu. Following the revelations, Israeli legislators call for a parliamentary investigation, Israel’s ministry of public security and Prime Minister Naftali Bennett pledge an investigation and the State Comptroller of Israel says he will expand his ongoing investigation into the use of surveillance technology by law enforcement to include the latest Pegasus allegations. A week after the Calcalist revelations, the chairman of NSO Group steps down from his position but says his departure was planned months ago and is unrelated to the scandal.

That same month, Israeli newspaper Haaretz reveals that NSO Group is in negotiations to sell the company to a US venture capital firm in the aftermath of being blacklisted by the US administration. The New York Times also discloses that the state of Israel provided a Pegasus license to Bahrain and the United Arab Emirates during the negotiation process of the Abraham Accords, a 2020 diplomatic pact to normalize relations between Israel and the Arab countries.

On February 1, 2022, the Israeli police admits it had misused Pegasus. On February 7, Calcalist reports that the Israeli police used Pegasus without a warrant against a former editor-in-chief of Israeli news outlet Walla, Walla journalists, chief executives, activists, political advisers and Netanyahu’s son Avner Netanyahu, among others. (Calcalist's revelations were denied by NSO Group, which sued the newspaper. Members of the Israeli government, such as Justice Minister Gideon Sa'ar, also contended the Calcalist findings and, according to an interim report, found "no indication" of hacking using Pegasus. Calcalist's revelations were also criticized by the Israeli media, which noted the lack of clear evidence provided by the business newspaper. Calcalist later said it stood by its revelations, while admitting that there may have been mistakes regarding the spyware’s victims.)

In the aftermath of Calcalist’s reports, Israel announces it will investigate its police force’s alleged use of Pegasus. A couple of days later, thousands of people gather in Tel Aviv to demand the establishment of an inquiry commission into the allegations that the police illegally spied on witnesses in the trial of former Prime Minister Benjamin Netanhayu.

In May 2022, the Knesset passes a preliminary bill calling for the establishment of a state inquiry into the allegations that the Israeli police conducted extrajudicial spying against dozens of citizens (the bill still needs to go through three more votes to become law).
