Luxembourg

In the wake of the murder of Saudi Arabian journalist and dissident Jamal Khashoggi in 2018, The Washington Post reports that Saudi Arabia conducted some of its transactions with NSO Group through one of the company’s Luxembourg-based entities, Q Cyber Technologies (a spokesperson for NSO Group and Q Cyber would neither confirm nor deny any of the firm’s clients). Questioned by a member of the Parliament on the matter, the government of Luxembourg says in February 2019 that it would consider “the possibility of legislation on the obligation for due diligence for companies registered in Luxembourg.”

In July 2021, the Pegasus Project points out that Luxembourg hosts the parent company of NSO Group, OSY.

Two days after the revelations, Luxembourg Minister of Foreign Affairs Jean Asselborn confirms that NSO Group has “two offices” in the Grand Duchy, but says they did not apply for export licenses of cybersurveillance products. The government of Luxembourg also says that NSO Group is represented by seven additional entities in Luxembourg and that there is no evidence that the phones of Luxembourgish citizens have been infiltrated using Pegasus. The same day, Asselborn addresses a letter to the managers of NSO Group’s Luxembourg-based entities to say that Luxembourg will not tolerate operations from them that would contribute to the violation of human rights abroad.

In October 2021, the Prime Minister of Luxembourg Xavier Bettel suggests during a live-streamed event that the Luxembourg government had bought Pegasus, saying that “when we bought it, it was for reasons of state security.” The Prime Minister backtracks three days later in an answer to parliamentary questions, stating that he was “referring in a general manner to the purchase of such a tool.

After the US ban of NSO Group, the government of Luxembourg says in late 2021 that the Grand Duchy cannot blacklist the company because its legislation “does not provide” for such a procedure.
