The FBI confirmed the purchase of a "limited license" of the spyware, but says there has been "no operational use in support of any investigation" and that it uses the software "for product testing and evaluation only."

In May 2019, American tech company WhatsApp discovers that a vulnerability in its messaging app has allowed NSO Group to target over 1,400 mobile phones using Pegasus. With assistance from Citizen Lab, the company identifies over 100 cases of targeting of human rights activists and journalists in at least 20 countries. According to WhatsApp, an American number is on the list. Judicial documents of the case mention that American citizens are on the list. In the absence of technical analyses, however, it is impossible to know whether the infection was successful. In an unprecedented stance from a major tech company, WhatsApp openly condemns the incident and sues NSO Group in the US for exploiting its platform. Major tech companies such as Microsoft, Google and LinkedIn file a legal brief in support of WhatsApp's complaint.

In 2020, Citizen Lab reveals the first known case of Pegasus targeting against an American citizen: New York Times journalist Ben Hubbard.

In July 2021, the Pegasus Project identifies nearly a dozen foreign numbers of U.S. citizens, including government officials, listed as persons of interest by NSO Group customers. The investigation further reveals that the U.S. number of a U.S. diplomat was on the list (NSO Group said it was made "technically impossible" to deploy Pegasus against U.S. numbers). According to the technical analysis of the investigation, it seems that this number was selected by the government of Morocco (Morocco denies having used Pegasus).

In response to the Pegasus Project, NSA whistleblower Edward Snowden and WhatsApp CEO Will Cathcart each call for a global moratorium on the trade and use of international surveillance technology. So too do 156 civil society organizations and 26 independent experts in a joint open letter to states, as well as UN human rights experts. Amazon Web Services shuts down infrastructure and accounts linked to NSO Group. On July 26, four prominent US lawmakers jointly issue a statement calling the Biden administration to take action against surveillance companies following the Pegasus Project. On July 29, the White House speaks with Israeli officials about spyware concerns. In September, UN High Commissioner for Human Rights Michelle Bachelet issues a statement to the Committee on Legal Affairs and Human Rights, Parliamentary Assembly Council of Europe, asking states not only to implement a moratorium on the sale and transfer of surveillance technology, but also to “rein in the surveillance industry.

In November, the US government blacklists NSO Group.

The same month, media outlets report that Apple revealed the existence of dozens of new victims around the world, who the company alerted individually. The tech company also announces that it filed a lawsuit against NSO Group.

In December, Reuters reports that Apple alerted several US embassy employees in Uganda (11 according to The Washington Post, who confirmed the information) that their iPhones had been hacked using Pegasus in the past months (Ugandan officials did not immediately reply to the journalists).

In January 2022, The New York Times reveals that the US Federal police had obtained Pegasus spyware and that it had once debated with the US Justice Department whether to deploy it for use in domestic law enforcement investigations (the FBI later confirmed the purchase of a "limited license" of the spyware, but said there has been "no operational use in support of any investigation" and that it uses the software "for product testing and evaluation only"). The Times also reveals that the CIA had purchased a Pegasus license for the government of Djibouti (Djibouti denied and the CIA declined to comment).

In February, the members of the Pegasus project disclose that the US Department of Justice launched a criminal investigation into NSO Group and that a whistleblower alleged that an NSO Group executive offered “bags of cash” to an US-based mobile security company in exchange for access to a global signaling network used to track mobile phones.

The New Yorker reveals in April that the Biden Administration is investigating “additional targeting of U.S. officials” and is considering banning the purchase or use of foreign commercial spyware by the US government, or others, if it has been “improperly used abroad.”

In June, The Guardian, Haaretz and The Washington Post report on discussions between a U.S. defense contractor, L3Harris, and NSO Group about a sale of the latter’s surveillance technology. A White House official says that "such a transaction, if it were to take place, raises serious counterintelligence and security concerns for the US government", which led L3Harris to abandon talks to purchase NSO Group’s surveillance technology, as the three media outlets report in July. According to the New York Times, several sources familiar with the negotiations said that American defense officials once supported L3Harris’ plans to purchase NSO Group.
