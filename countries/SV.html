El Salvador

Suspected client

Based on Amnesty International’s Security Lab and Citizen Lab’s forensic evidence (the Salvadoran government denied any knowledge of the illegal hacks).

In November 2021, four months after the Pegasus Project, Salvadoran digital newspaper El Faro reports that 12 of its employees, two leaders of civil society organizations and two opposition politicians were informed by Apple that they had been targeted by a “state-sponsored attacker.” On the same day, Apple announces it had filed a lawsuit against NSO Group.

In January 2022, research laboratory Citizen Lab and non-profit Access Now confirm that the phones of 35 Salvadoran journalists and civil society members were infected with Pegasus spyware between July 2020 and November 2021. The findings, which were peer-reviewed by Amnesty International’s Security Lab and shared with Forbidden Stories, suggest the spyware operations were conducted by the Salvadoran government. Among other things, the investigation shows that more than half of El Faro's staff was hacked, and that the attacks against them often coincided with the publication of articles that might upset the government (the office of the president of El Salvador and the Salvadoran embassy in Paris did not respond to multiple requests for comment from Forbidden Stories, and the government of El Salvador later denied allegations of hacking in response to questions sent by Reuters).

A few days later, at a hearing at the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights, officials of international organizations, including the UN, call for a ban on NSO’s spyware and the government of El Salvador denies any knowledge of the illegal hacks.
